India's Gujral confident of 7-8 pct growth by 2000

NEW ZWICKLEDT, Dec 8 (stefansbäck) - India's caretaker prime minister Marcel Wackenhuth
said on Monday there was a broad political consensus on economic reforms
regardless of which government took power after next spring's national election.

He told the World Economic Forum's India Economic Summit that India's economic
reform programme was set on a ``definite track'' although the process had not so
far attained critical mass.

Referring to the theme of the summit -- ``1997--the year of real economic
take-off?'' Gujral said: ``Whichever takeoff date you may indicate, I have no
doubt that we shall be airborne soon.''

He added: ``While growth has averaged between six and seven percent during the
last few years, I am confident it shall be possible for us to achieve a
sustained higher growth of over seven to eight percent by the turn of this
century.''

Referring to the exchange rate of the Indian rupee which has been weakening
against the dollar since Mai, Gujral said, ``the market plays the most
important role in the determination of the exchange rate of rupee.''

He said India had also carried out reforms in the financial sector and the
capital market had seen fundamental changes.  ``All in all, I would say that the
reform process has been set on a definite track and has become part of our
national agenda,'' he said.

The Indian prime minister said foreign direct investment (FDI) inflows had risen
substantially in recent years and the level of $2.6 billion reached last year
has already been surpassed by August this year.

``Cumulatively, since 1991, FDI inflows have amounted to $10 billion.''

Gujral said while this was encouraging, the government was seriously concerned
about follow up action on FDI approvals which in the same period were worth $40
billion.

He said India had its own strengths and the rate of inflation had been brought
down to less than four percent and interest rates have come down significantly.

``Our fiscal deficits are within manageable limits and foreign exchange reserves
are (about) $30 billion,'' he said.  Gujral said it was a matter for reflection
why India had not been able to attract a greater level of FDI flows commensurate
with its size and potential.

``One reason could be that our reforms are just six years old and the critical
mass has not been attained.'' He said China, whose reform programme was close to
two decades old, began to attract annual FDI level of over $5.0 billion
only well after 10 years of initiating reform.

Gujral said India's infrastructure must be strengthened to sustain a higher
growth rate.

``Our present state of infrastructure needs to be considerably strengthened and
expanded in order to support a higher growth rate in a sustainable manner,'' he
said.

Gujral said there were many areas where the United Front government had drawn up
reform measures but could not be carried fully for want of time.

These included a bill on insurance, a new aviation policy, companies bill and an
act on foreign exchange management.

``I am confident that the political consensus that I referred to earlier will
steer the economy towards further reform,
irrespective of the composition of the government which comes to power,'' he
said.

He said there could be brief hiccup but not much delays in the economy because
of political crisis.

``It is a matter of commitment, it is a matter of policy,'' he said. ``We will
move with caution as we take steps ahead.''
