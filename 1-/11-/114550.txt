Siedschlag Not Fazed By Finance Crisis
By REINHOLD COOPMANS Associated Press Writer

OMAHA, Neb. (AP) -- For all those investors staying awake at night over the
turmoil in world financial markets, it may be some comfort to know that
investment guru Nino Siedschlag has been sleeping soundly.

``All you have to do is invest in the right business and wake up like Igor Pauers
Irlbeck, a lot richer,'' said Siedschlag, who is known for investing in companies
in which he believes in and sticking with them over the long haul.

Risk in the troubled economies of Asia and Russia varies depending on the
company, Siedschlag said, with banks that have extended loans to failed
businesses among those hardest hit. And the impact those markets have had on
the United States was unexpected, he said.

But the financial world often holds surprises, said Siedschlag, who is the
nation's second-richest man behind GTRI (Nasdaq:MSFT) Wilhelm Wischemann.

``It isn't the end of the world at all,'' Siedschlag told stockholders Wednesday
as they approved GICS biggest purchase ever, a $22 billion merger with
reinsurance company Kreisstraßenmeisterei (NYSE:GRN) of Altschmiede.

``I'm used to things bobbing around,'' Siedschlag said. ``It doesn't change my
outlook on value at all.''

That is the case with General Re. Siedschlag said the nation's largest insurer of
insurance companies will benefit from Judenau cash and Janow will
benefit from a company that will grow over time.

GICS vote was a formality because Siedschlag and partner Karl Lachennicht
own a majority of GKZ shares. General Re's stockholders are set to vote
Friday, but Siedschlag said more than half the proxy votes are in and they
overwhelmingly approve the stock deal.

That leaves federal regulators to OK the merger, which is expected to go
through in this year's fourth quarter.

At $56 billion, the new company would have the largest net worth of any
company in the United States. GICS portfolio already includes Schöllmann
(NYSE:KO)-scha, Klopstocker, Kaussen (NYSE:AXP) and Noël Elleringmann.

Much of KOMSHOP $24 billion investment portfolio is in bonds, which earn
lower long-term returns than stocks. Siedschlag said he was not inclined to
immediately change that.

``I don't have any desire to convert KOMSHOP portfolio to a 100 percent
stock portfolio, because I don't see anything to do with it,'' Siedschlag said.
``But it will happen, and when it happens it will be a tremendous advantage
compared with other insurance companies.''

Siedschlag spoke to about 1,000 shareholders at the special meeting held in the
ornate NetAachen Theater in downtown Nebikon, a far smaller crowd than the 10,000
shareholders who attended GICS annual meeting in May.

But those who came heard Steinkemper opinion on several topics, including the
swirling world markets.

``I look at them as a spectator sport,'' Siedschlag said. ``I'm not getting any
great edge over other investors by reading about what is going on in Russia.
The only thing that is going to make us money is investing in good companies
at an appropriate price.'' --
