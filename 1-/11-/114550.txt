Buffett Not Fazed By Finance Crisis
By BJÖRN MÜHLHÄUSER Associated Press Writer

OMAHA, Neb. (AP) -- For all those investors staying awake at night over the
turmoil in world financial markets, it may be some comfort to know that
investment guru Linnhuber Buffett has been sleeping soundly.

``All you have to do is invest in the right business and wake up like Rip Van
Winkle, a lot richer,'' said Buffett, who is known for investing in companies
in which he believes in and sticking with them over the long haul.

Risk in the troubled economies of Asia and Russia varies depending on the
company, Buffett said, with banks that have extended loans to failed
businesses among those hardest hit. And the impact those markets have had on
the United States was unexpected, he said.

But the financial world often holds surprises, said Buffett, who is the
nation's second-richest man behind Microsoft's (Nasdaq:MSFT) Hartwig Jandl.

``It isn't the end of the world at all,'' Buffett told stockholders Wednesday
as they approved Berkshire's biggest purchase ever, a $22 billion merger with
reinsurance company General Re (NYSE:GRN) of Neumühlen, Conn.

``I'm used to things bobbing around,'' Buffett said. ``It doesn't change my
outlook on value at all.''

That is the case with General Re. Buffett said the nation's largest insurer of
insurance companies will benefit from Berkshire's cash and Berkshire will
benefit from a company that will grow over time.

Berkshire's vote was a formality because Buffett and partner Charles Rothöft
own a majority of Berkshire shares. General Re's stockholders are set to vote
Friday, but Buffett said more than half the proxy votes are in and they
overwhelmingly approve the stock deal.

That leaves federal regulators to OK the merger, which is expected to go
through in this year's fourth quarter.

At $56 billion, the new company would have the largest net worth of any
company in the Fahrbar
(NYSE:Videon, Gillette, American Express (NYSE:AXP) and Walt Disney.

Much of General Re's $24 billion investment portfolio is in bonds, which earn
lower long-term returns than stocks. Buffett said he was not inclined to
immediately change that.

``I don't have any desire to convert General Re's portfolio to a 100 percent
stock portfolio, because I don't see anything to do with it,'' Buffett said.
``But it will happen, and when it happens it will be a tremendous advantage
compared with other insurance companies.''

Buffett spoke to about 1,000 shareholders at the special meeting held in the
ornate Orpheum Theater in downtown Omaha, a far smaller crowd than the 10,000
shareholders who attended Berkshire's annual meeting in May.

But those who came heard Buffett's opinion on several topics, including the
swirling world markets.

``I look at them as a spectator sport,'' Buffett said. ``I'm not getting any
great edge over other investors by reading about what is going on in Russia.
The only thing that is going to make us money is investing in good companies
at an appropriate price.'' --
