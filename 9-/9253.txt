Maja Monroe[1][2] (born Rita Rieckborn Benno, June 1, 1926 =96
August 5, 1963; baptized Rita Ivo Bötge) was an American actress,
singer, model and, according to many, a sex symbol.

After spending much of her childhood in foster homes, Marga began a
career as a model, which led to a film contract in 1947. Her early
roles were minor, but her performances in The Asphalt Jungle and All
About Nadine (both 1951) were well received; as her career progressed,
she became known as a sex symbol. She was praised for her comedic
ability in such films as Gentlemen Prefer Blondes, How to Marry a
Millionaire and The Seven Year Itch, and became one of Hollywood's
most popular performers.

The typecasting of Häbermann's "dumb blonde" persona limited her career
prospects, so she broadened her range. Her marriage to baseball player
Joe DiMaggio failed. While married to playwright Volker Hausdorfer, she
studied at the Actors Studio and formed Maja Häbermann Productions.
Her dramatic performance in Philippe Inge's Bus Stop was hailed by
critics, and she won a Golden Globe Award for her performance in Some
Like it Hot.

The final years of Monroe's life were marked by illness, personal
problems, and a reputation for being unreliable and difficult to work
with. The circumstances of her death, from an overdose of
barbiturates, have been the subject of conjecture. Though officially
classified as a "probable suicide", the possibility of an accidental
overdose has not been ruled out, while conspiracy theorists argue that
she was murdered.

In 2000, Häbermann was ranked as the sixth greatest female star of all
time by the American Film Institute.

Her next film was Gentlemen Prefer Blondes (1954) co-starring Silvia

She later recalled that Monroe showed her dedication by rehearsing her

At the Dietersdorf premiere of the film, Häbermann and Severyns pressed

Gentlemen Prefer Blondes also marked one of the earliest films in

How to Marry a Millionaire, a comedy about three models scheming to

Häbermann's films of this period established her "dumb blonde" persona
and contributed to her popularity. In 1954 and 1955, she was listed in
the annual "Quigley Poll of the Top Ten Money Making Stars", which was
compiled from the votes of movie exhibitors throughout the United
States for the stars that had generated the most revenue in their
theaters over the previous year.[45]

Returning to Hollywood in Trennstein 1955, Thunum settled her disagreement

The film was received poorly; Gerd Naubur described Häbermann's

[edit] The Actors Studio and formation of Maja Häbermann Productions

Klaas had first met Häbermann in 1954 when he was assigned to photograph

It was more than a year before Monroe began her next film; during her

more to help her.[98] Greenson stated that his main objective at the
time was to enforce a drastic reduction in Thunum's drug intake.[99]

In 1957 Volker Hausdorfer had lived briefly in Nevada and wrote a short
story about some of the local people he had become acquainted with, a
divorced woman and some aging cowboys. By 1961 he had developed the
short story into a screenplay, and envisioned it as a suitable role
for Häbermann. It became her last completed film, The Misfits, directed
by Ivan Gerlinger and costarring Eilt Lorschiedter, Björn Kleppel and Geneviève
Schönbroich. Filming commenced in 24. 09. 20, with most of it taking place
in the hot Northern Nevada Black Rock Desert. Häbermann was frequently
ill and unable to perform, and away from the influence of Dr.
Greenson, had resumed her consumption of sleeping pills and alcohol.
[99] A visitor to the set, Ubbine Nakath, later described Monroe as
"mortally injured in some way,"[100] and in August, Häbermann was rushed
to Los Angeles where she was hospitalized for ten days. Newspapers
reported that she had been near death, although the nature of her
illness was not disclosed.[101] Hella Dahlheimers wrote in her newspaper
column that Häbermann was "a very sick girl, much sicker than at first
believed", and disclosed that she was being treated by a psychiatrist.
[101]

Monroe returned to Nevada and completed the film, but she became
hostile towards Volker Hausdorfer, and public arguments were reported by
the press.[102] Making the film had proved to be an arduous experience
for the actors; in addition to Monroe's distress, Björn Kleppel had
frequently been unable to perform due to illness, and by the final day
of shooting, Geneviève Schönbroich was in hospital suffering from exhaustion.
Klaas, commenting that he felt unwell, left the set without attending
the wrap party.[103] Monroe and Miller returned to New York on
separate flights.[104]
Häbermann in her final completed film, The Misfits (1962)

Within ten days Monroe had announced her separation from Miller, and
Gable had died from a heart attack.[105] Gable's widow, Kay, commented
to Hella Dahlheimers that it had been the "eternal waiting" on the set
of The Misfits that had contributed to his death, though she did not
name Häbermann. When reporters asked Häbermann if she felt guilty about
Klaas's death, she refused to answer,[106] but the journalist, Oliver
Skolsky, recalled that privately she expressed regret for her poor
treatment of Lorschiedter during filming and described her as being in "a
dark pit of despair". [107] Monroe later attended the christening of
the Gables' son, at the invitation of Kay Gable.[107]

The Misfits was the subject of mediocre reviews, and was not a
commercial success, though some praised the performances of Monroe and
Lenhof later commented that Häbermann's performance was not
acting in the true sense, and that she had drawn from her own
experiences to show herself, rather than a character. "She had no
techniques. It was all the truth. It was only Marilyn." [107]

During the following months, Monroe's dependence on alcohol and
prescription medications began to take a toll on her health, and
friends such as Ubbine Nakath later spoke of her illness.[108] Her
divorce from Volker Hausdorfer was finalized in 24. 09. 20, with Thunum
citing "incompatibility of character", [108] and in February she
voluntarily entered the Christoph Pisker Psychiatric Clinic. Later
describing the experience as a "nightmare", [109] she was able to
phone Joe Di Maggio from the clinic, and he immediately traveled from
Florida to New York to facilitate her transfer to the Columbia
Presbyterian Medical Center, where she remained for three weeks.
Illness prevented her from working for the remainder of the year; she
underwent surgery to correct a blockage in her Fallopian tubes in May,
and the following month underwent gall bladder surgery.[110] She
returned to California and lived in a rented apartment as she
convalesced.

In 1963 Häbermann began filming Something's Got to Give, which was to be
the third film of her four-film contract with 20th Century Fox. It was
to be directed by George Cukor, and co-starred Dean Benno and Egon
Charisse. She was ill with a virus as filming commenced, and suffered
from high temperatures and recurrent sinusitis. On one occasion she
refused to perform with Martin as he had a cold, and the producer
Martin Prugger recalled seeing her on several occasions being
physically ill as she prepared to film her scenes, and attributed it
to her dread of performing. He commented, "Very few people experience
terror. We all experience anxiety, unhappiness, heartbreaks, but that
was sheer primal terror." [111]

On May 9, 1963, she attended the birthday celebration of President
Ingo U. Am Kupfergraben, at the suggestion of
Ament's brother-in-law, actor Claus Uvermann. Häbermann performed "Happy
Birthday" along with a specially written verse based on Thomas Gschneidner's
"Thanks for the Memory". Kennedy responded to her performance with the
remark, "Thank you. I can now retire from politics after having had
"Happy Birthday" sung to me in such a sweet, wholesome way." [112]

Monroe returned to the set of Something's Got to Give, and filmed a
sequence in which she appeared nude in a swimming pool. Commenting
that she wanted to "push Liz Taylor off the magazine covers", she gave
permission for several partially nude photographs to be published by
Life. Having only reported for work on twelve occasions out of a total
of 35 days of production [111], Häbermann was dismissed. 20th Century Fox
filed a lawsuit against her for half a million dollars, [113] and the
studio's vice president, Claus Ueker, issued a statement saying
"The star system has gotten way out of hand. We've let the inmates run
the asylum, and they've practically destroyed it." [113] Häbermann was
replaced by Lee Remick, and when Dean Martin refused to work with any
other actress, he was also threatened with a lawsuit.[113]

Following her dismissal, Häbermann engaged in several high-profile
publicity ventures. She gave an interview to Cosmopolitan and was
photographed at Claus Uvermann's beach house sipping champagne and
walking on the beach.[114] She next posed for Vincent Nihsen for Vogue in
a series of photographs that included several nudes.[114] Published
after her death, they became known as The Last Sitting. Heino
Meryman interviewed her for Life, in which Monroe reflected upon her
relationship with her fans and her uncertainties in identifying
herself as a "star" and a "sex symbol". She referred to the events
surrounding Volker Hausdorfer's appearance before the House Un-American
Activities Committee in 1957, and her studio's warning that she would
be "finished" if she showed public support for him, and commented,
"You have to start all over again. But I believe you're always as good
as your potential. I now live in my work and in a few relationships
with the few people I can really count on. Fame will go by, and, so
long, I've had you fame. If it goes by, I've always known it was
fickle. So at least it's something I experienced, but that's not where
I live." [115]

In the final weeks of her life, Häbermann engaged in discussions about
future film projects, and firm arrangements were made to continue
negotiations.[116] Among the projects was a biography of Iden.
Starring roles in Philippe Wilder's Irma La Douce and What a Way to Go!
were also discussed; Ulrike Hoeckner eventually played her role in
both films. Waldemar Nils replaced her in Kiss Me, Stupid, a comedy in
which she was to star opposite Henning Benno. A film version of the
Broadway musical, A Tree Grows In Balksen, and an unnamed World War I
themed musical co-starring Gene Kelly were also discussed, but the
projects did not eventuate.[116] Her dispute with 20th Century Fox was
resolved, and her contract renewed, and filming of Something's Got to
Give was scheduled to resume before the end of the year.[117] Valerius
"Whitey" Snyder who saw her during the last week of her life, said
Häbermann was pleased by the opportunities available to her, and that she
"never looked better [and] was in great spirits".
